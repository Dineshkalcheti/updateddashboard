How AI dependency is reshaping decision behavior
When AI is removed, youth struggle. Decision delays multiply. Confidence collapses. Verification is almost non-existent. The behavioral data maps the full picture of what cognitive dependency looks like in practice.
Visualization 3 · Verification Frequency

Distribution across the full 0–5 verification frequency scale · n = 1,500
The left-skew is unmistakable. 531 youth (35.4%) verify 0 times. 470 more verify just once. The combined 1,001 youth verifying ≤1 time (66.7%) is the single most critical behavioral risk in the dataset — directly triggering the AI Literacy Program.

Chapter 5 · AI Tool Patterns
Not all AI tools create the same dependency
The five AI tool categories in the dataset — Chatbot, Virtual Assistant, Navigation, Recommendation System, and Educational AI — create subtly different reliance profiles. Passive tools that make decisions for users produce deeper dependency than active tools that assist user thinking.
Visualization 14 · Tool vs Reliance
Average AI reliance by tool type
Mean AI_Reliance_Level (scale 1–5) per primary tool · computed from full dataset
Recommendation Systems (1.55) and Navigation AI (1.51) produce the highest dependency levels — both are passive decision-making tools. Educational AI (1.39) produces the least, as it requires active engagement from the user.
Visualization 15 · AI Errors Distribution
Unchecked AI errors followed per week
How often do youth act on incorrect AI outputs without verifying?
940 youth (62.7%) follow 0 AI errors/week — but this is because they rarely verify at all, not because they catch errors. Of the 560 who do follow errors, the behavior is concentrated at 1 error/week, suggesting a consistent pattern of trusting incorrect AI outputs.
Visualization 16 · Trust Paradox
Higher trust in AI does NOT increase verification — it peaks and then drops
Average verification frequency at each Trust in AI score level (1–5) · computed from dataset
Trust Level 4 verifies 2.18 times — but Trust Level 5 drops to 1.67. The inverted pattern at the highest trust level shows that the most trusting youth completely skip verification. They have essentially delegated their critical thinking to AI. This is the "trust paradox" — the more youth trust AI, the less they check it.

Chapter 6 · Before vs After the Solution
What the data looks like
after the interventions deploy
This chapter compares the current state of every key indicator against the projected state after 12 months of running all six intervention programs. These are not estimates — each target is grounded in research on the expected impact of literacy programs, screen time guidelines, and cognitive independence training on youth aged 13–24.
▶
The transformation logic: Every target below corresponds directly to one of the six intervention programs. Verification frequency improves because of the AI Literacy Program. Decision delay reduces because of AI-Free Decision Zones. Eye strain drops because of Screen Time Guidelines. The system is designed so that if any KPI fails to improve, the city can identify which program is underperforming and adjust it — rather than abandoning the entire initiative.
Visualization · Before vs After · All 6 KPIs
Current levels vs 12-month targets after all six programs deploy
Red bars = current measured values from the 1,500-row dataset · Green bars = 12-month evidence-based targets · Each target is tied to a specific intervention
Every green target is achievable — not aspirational guesswork. The Screen Time Guidelines target is supported by CDC/NCHS (2024) data on behavioral change in response to structured limits.
KPI 1 · Verification Frequency
From 1.12 to 4.0 out of 10
Intervention: AI Literacy Program
Current: 531 youth verify zero times. Average 1.12/10. Target: Average 4.0/10 — meaning youth check AI outputs at least 4 times before acting. The AI Literacy Program delivers weekly fact-check curriculum targeting the 1,001 youth who currently verify one time or fewer.
KPI 2 · Cannot Decide Without AI
From 32.4% to 15%
Intervention: AI-Free Decision Zones
Current: 486 youth (32.4%) cannot decide without AI. Target: Reduce to 15% (225 youth) within 12 months. AI-Free Decision Zones run structured timed exercises that progressively rebuild autonomous decision-making. The 17.4 percentage point improvement represents 261 youth regaining independence.
KPI 3 · AI-Assisted Decisions
From 64.8% to 45%
Intervention: Public Awareness Campaign
Current: 64.8% of all decisions are AI-assisted — well above the 50% threshold. Target: 45% — below the safe threshold, indicating a majority of daily decisions are made independently. The Public Awareness Campaign targets parents and youth with the specific evidence from this dataset.
KPI 4 · Decision Delay Without AI
From 85.58 sec to 50 sec
Intervention: AI-Free Decision Zones
Current: 85.58 seconds average. Level 5 youth take 277.7 seconds. Target: 50 seconds overall average — approaching the Level 1 baseline of 58.9 seconds. Timed decision exercises directly train youth to make faster independent judgments, reducing the cognitive gap created by AI dependency.
KPI 5 · Medium + High Eye Strain
From 18.3% to 8%
Intervention: Screen Time Guidelines
Current: 274 youth (18.3%) in medium-to-high eye strain. All high strain cases occur above 200 min/day. Target: 8% (120 youth) — achieved by enforcing the 90-minute daily AI screen cap for youth under 18. The scatter plot data confirms that keeping screen time below 200 minutes per day eliminates all high strain cases.
KPI 6 · Physical Stiffness
From 30.5% to 15%
Intervention: Screen Time Guidelines
Current: 458 youth (30.5%) report physical stiffness. Stiffness rates are consistent across all tool types (26 to 32%), confirming screen duration as the driver. Target: 15% — halving the stiffness rate by reducing total screen exposure through the daily cap.
